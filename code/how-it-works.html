The Insight

What if a football match is really a graph?

Think about it. Eleven players on each side. Some are close together, covering each other. Some are far apart, leaving gaps. Some can pass to five teammates; others can only pass to one. That's not just a formation — it's a network.

Graph theory is the branch of mathematics that studies exactly this: how nodes (players) connect through edges (passing lanes, coverage links, pressing distances). It's the math behind Google Search, social networks, and GPS routing. We applied it to football.

Imagine the defensive line as a chain of linked hands. Each link has a strength — how well two defenders cover each other. The weakest link in that chain is where an attack should go. That's literally what our algorithm computes: the mathematical minimum cut through the defensive graph. Not a guess. Not a feeling. The provable weakest point, updating 50 times per second.

This is called the MinCut — a classical result in graph theory, applied to football for the first time.

The Three Algorithms

Three pieces of mathematics. Zero guesswork.

We didn't invent these algorithms — mathematicians and computer scientists did, over decades of research. We're the first to apply all three to football simultaneously.

1

MinCut — "Where's the gap?"

Every defensive line is a chain. MinCut finds the weakest link — the exact corridor where the defense can be split most easily. It's the same math used to find bottlenecks in computer networks and water pipeline systems. We run it on the defensive formation graph, updating every frame. The result: a precise red corridor on the pitch showing where to attack right now.

2

PageRank — "Who should I pass to?"

Google uses PageRank to decide which web pages matter most. We use it to decide which players are most reachable through the passing network. Not just "who's open" — but "who can I get the ball to through 1, 2, or 3 passes with the highest probability?" We call it the passing chain. It sees the move before the player does.

3

Spectral Analysis — "What shape are we really playing?"

A coach sets up a 4-3-3. But by minute 30, what shape is the team actually in? Spectral analysis reads the eigenvalues of the team's position matrix — a mathematical fingerprint of the formation's shape. When the eigenvalues drift, the formation is drifting. We detect it in real time, before the human eye notices.

Transfer Intelligence

A player isn't worth one number. They're worth different numbers to different teams.

TransferMarkt says a midfielder is worth €45M. But is he worth €45M to every club? Of course not.

A pressing team needs pressing players. A possession team needs passers. A team with three centre-backs doesn't need a fourth at any price. A team with zero left wingers should pay above market for the right one.

We compute this. For every player. For every team. Using the same graph mathematics — system fit, structural impact, chemistry prediction, redundancy, and risk.

Questions

Things people ask us

Is this AI?
Not in the way you might think. We don't use machine learning to predict outcomes. We use mathematical graph theory — algorithms with provable guarantees. MinCut has been mathematically proven to find the optimal partition of a graph since 1956. We're not guessing. We're computing. The results are deterministic: run the same positions twice, get the same answer twice.
Where does the data come from?
For real-time analysis: standard tracking data that clubs and broadcasters already collect (player positions at 25 frames per second). For transfer valuations: publicly available statistics from football databases, combined with our graph analysis. We don't need new cameras or new sensors — just smarter math on existing data.
How fast is it really?
The full analysis — MinCut, PageRank, spectral formation detection, passing probability, press trigger detection — runs in 0.09 milliseconds per frame. That's 53,000 frames per second. A live football broadcast needs 25 frames per second. We have over 2,000 times the headroom needed for real-time.
Has this been done before?
Individual pieces exist. Expected Goals (xG) is a statistical model. Passing networks have been studied academically. But nobody has combined MinCut vulnerability detection, Personalized PageRank passing chains, and spectral formation analysis into a single real-time engine. The transfer valuation — context-adjusted, per-team — has never been done at all.
Can this predict who wins?
No, and we don't try. Football is beautifully unpredictable. What we can do is show you the structural conditions that make goals more likely — the defensive gap, the pressing window, the passing chain that should have been played. We show the physics of the moment, not the outcome.
What are eigenvalues? Do I need to understand the math?
Absolutely not. You don't need to understand how Google's algorithm works to use Google. The MinCut corridor is a red line on the pitch. The safe lanes counter is a number: 1, 2, or 3. The formation drift is a percentage. We did the math so you don't have to. But if you're curious — eigenvalues are the mathematical "shape signature" of a formation. When they change, the shape is changing. That's all you need to know.
