How PitchIntel Works

Ok Football Nerds,
Let's Talk.

You know TransferMarkt. You know the rumor mill. You know some player is "worth €45 million" because... someone said so? Because comparable players sold for that? Because vibes?

Here's the thing nobody says out loud: a player isn't worth one number. They're worth different numbers to different teams. A pressing monster is worth €60M to Liverpool and €25M to a team that sits deep. A creative 10 is worth twice as much to a team with no playmaker as it is to a team that already has three.

Everyone in football knows this intuitively. We're the first to compute it.

Here's how.

The Core Idea

Every player is a point in 118-dimensional space

Bear with us — this is the part that sounds complicated but is actually simple.

You already think of players in multiple dimensions. You might think: "he's fast, good passer, doesn't press much, great in the air, injury prone." That's five dimensions. You just described a point in 5D space.

We do the same thing, but with 118 dimensions. Passing accuracy at short, medium, and long range. Expected goals per shot. Pressing frequency in each third of the pitch. Sprint speed, distance covered, how many teammates they connect with, how the team's defensive structure changes when they're on the pitch versus off it.

We call this the player vector. It's a mathematical fingerprint of everything a player does — and everything they are.

The technical term

In machine learning and data science, this is called a vector embedding — a way of representing something complex (a player's total contribution to football) as a single point in a high-dimensional space. Players who play similarly end up near each other in this space. Players who are different end up far apart. Distance in this space has meaning.

But here's where it gets interesting. The vector isn't just about the player. It's about the player in context.

The Key Insight

Value = how well your vector fits their graph

Every team is also a mathematical structure. Not just a list of players — a network. Who passes to whom. Where the defensive coverage overlaps. Where the gaps are. Which player everything flows through. We call this the team graph.

When you drop a player's vector into a team's graph, you can measure the fit. Does their passing profile strengthen or weaken the network? Does their defensive positioning fill a structural gap or duplicate existing coverage? Does their playing style create new connections or leave dead zones?

The answer is different for every team. Mathematically, provably different.

The Five Factors

What goes into a context valuation

Every valuation is the sum of five structural factors. No opinions. No gut feelings. Just the math of how a player's vector interacts with a team's graph.

1. System Fit

Does the player's style match the team's system? A pressing team needs pressing players. A possession team needs passers. A counter-attacking team needs pace. We match the player's 118-dimensional profile against the team's tactical fingerprint — formation, build-up style, defensive height, pressing intensity. The more dimensions align, the higher the fit.

2. Structural Impact

Does the player fix a structural weakness? Every team has a vulnerable zone — a place where the defensive graph is weakest. (We compute this using something called minimum cut theory — the mathematical proof of where a network can be split most easily.) If the player's positional profile fills that exact gap, their value goes up. Dramatically.

3. Chemistry

How does the player connect to existing teammates? Some players create new pathways through the passing network. Others duplicate existing ones. Using a technique called PageRank (the same math Google uses to rank web pages), we measure how central a player would be in the team's network — and whether they'd reduce dangerous over-reliance on a single hub player.

4. Redundancy

This one's simple but powerful. If the team already has three centre-backs and zero left wingers, a centre-back is worth less to them than the market says, and a left winger is worth more. Everyone knows this intuitively — we just quantify how much.

5. Risk & Upside

Age, contract length, injury history, league transition risk. A 21-year-old on a long contract has built-in upside that inflates their context value. A 30-year-old with two years left has built-in risk that deflates it. Cross-league moves carry friction — same-league transfers carry a rivalry premium. The model accounts for all of it.

The five factor scores combine into a single multiplier. Market value × multiplier = context value for that specific team.

A multiplier above 1.2x means the player is worth more to this team than the market thinks. Below 0.8x means they'd be overpaying. It's that simple.

The 118 Dimensions

Three tiers of data. Each one makes the valuation sharper.

Not all 118 dimensions are equal. They come from different sources, at different levels of availability. The more you feed the model, the more it sees.

Tier 1 — Public Data (57 dimensions)

Everything available from public football statistics: passing accuracy at three distance ranges, expected goals, expected assists, progressive carries, tackles, interceptions, pressing actions per zone, sprint counts, aerial duels, dribble success, and more. Available for every professional footballer. Powers the free tier.

Tier 2 — Positional Intelligence (36 dimensions)

Where a player actually operates on the pitch. How much space they create with their movement. Their centrality in the passing network. How the team's defensive structure changes when they play. Their performance under pressure — when winning, losing, in the last 15 minutes, against top teams. Requires positional tracking data. Powers the pro tier.

Tier 3 — Your Eyes (25 dimensions)

The stuff algorithms can't see on their own. Culture fit. Coachability. Dressing room effect. How they handle media pressure. Whether their agent is a nightmare. Hold-up play quality for strikers. Organizational voice for defenders. You add these. You've watched the player. You know things the data doesn't. When you tell the model, the valuation shifts — and it shifts differently for every team.

This is the tier that makes your valuations yours. No two users see the same numbers, because no two people have the same scouting eyes.

Why this is different from scouting platforms

Scouting platforms show you player stats and let you filter. "Show me all left wingers under 25 with more than 5 progressive carries per 90." That's a spreadsheet query.

We answer a fundamentally different question: "Which specific player would improve THIS team's structural graph the most?" Two teams can run the same query and get completely different answers. Because the answer depends on the team, not just the player.

Beyond Players

Coaches have vectors too

The same approach works for managers. Every coach imposes a tactical fingerprint on their team — a preferred possession %, pressing intensity, defensive line height, build-up speed, network structure, player development rate.

We represent this as a coach vector. Then we match it against the team's current graph and the squad's player profiles. The result: a fit score that predicts how much friction a managerial appointment would create.

The Honest Bit

What we can't do (yet)

We believe in being straight with you. Here's what PitchIntel is and isn't.

We don't predict match results. Football is gloriously unpredictable. We analyze structural conditions, not outcomes. We can tell you the defense has a gap on the left side. We can't tell you a goal will come from it.

Our free-tier data is estimated, not measured. Tier 1 valuations are based on public statistics converted into graph metrics through calibrated models. They're good — significantly better than a single market number — but they're not as precise as Tier 2 (positional tracking data) or Tier 3 (your scouting eyes). We tell you the confidence level on every valuation so you know how much to trust it.

Football isn't just math. A player could score perfectly on every dimension and still fail because of something no vector captures — a family situation, a personality clash, a city they hate, a manager who doesn't believe in them. The model gives you the structural truth. The human truth is still your job.

We'd rather you trust us at 80% than oversell and lose you at 100%. The model gets better as we add dimensions, as you add your scouting data, and as we incorporate more data sources. But the core insight — context changes value — is mathematically sound today.

FAQ

Questions we get

What's a vector embedding? Is this AI?
A vector embedding is a way of representing something complex as a list of numbers. Think of GPS coordinates — two numbers (latitude, longitude) represent a location on Earth. A player vector is 118 numbers representing a player's complete football profile. Players who play similarly have similar vectors. Players who are different have different vectors.

As for AI: we use mathematical graph theory, not machine learning. The algorithms have provable guarantees — run them twice on the same data, get the same answer. No hallucination, no black boxes, no "the model thinks." Just math.
How is the custom scouting data used?
When you add Tier 3 dimensions (culture fit, coachability, etc.), those values are weighted 2x more than public stats because they're more differentiating. Your assessments are private — no other user sees them. They're combined with the base valuation in real time: drag a slider, watch the number change. Your custom data is stored in your account and applied to every valuation you compute.
Where does the data come from?
Tier 1 data comes from public football statistics databases, refreshed weekly. Market values come from established valuation sources. Squad depth is compiled from public squad listings. Formation and tactical style data are derived from match-level statistics. We don't scrape anything private — this is all publicly available information, analyzed in a way nobody else does.
Can I use this for betting?
PitchIntel is a structural analysis tool, not a betting product. That said, understanding which teams have structural advantages is useful context for anyone trying to understand football more deeply. We don't make predictions or recommend bets.
How often are valuations updated?
The underlying stats are refreshed weekly during the season and daily during transfer windows. The gem scanner re-runs automatically with each data refresh. If a player's stats change significantly (injury, form drop, new contract), their vector changes, and every valuation that includes them recalculates.
What's the minimum cut thing about?
A minimum cut (MinCut) is a concept from graph theory. Imagine a defensive line as a chain — each link is the coverage between two adjacent defenders. The MinCut is the weakest link: the place where you need to remove the least total coverage to split the chain in two. In football terms, it's the corridor through the defense where an attack is most likely to succeed. We compute this in real time and use it to evaluate whether a transfer target would strengthen or weaken a team's defensive graph.
Is this just for nerds? Can a regular fan use this?
The interface is designed so anyone can use it: pick a club, see the gems, done. You never have to see a vector or understand graph theory. But if you want to go deeper — if you want to understand why Xavi Simons is worth €115M to Atletico and €84M to City — the factor breakdowns are right there. Go as deep as you want.
